\begin{abstract}
Forecasting dynamic financial co-movements requires integrating asynchronous heterogeneous signals into a continuous-time relational model. We propose DyFO (Dynamic Financial Observer), a non-recurrent Temporal Graph Attention Network for next-day pairwise correlation forecasting over a heterogeneous financial graph with three typed edge classes: DCC-GARCH dynamic correlations (CORR), GICS sector links (SECT), and Fama--French five-factor co-loadings (FACT). A key contribution is a relation-aware graph attention layer that conditions attention weights on typed edge embeddings, preventing the homogeneous treatment of structurally heterogeneous neighbours and mitigating the attention-dilution failure of standard single-type graph attention.Evaluated on 50 S\&P 500 constituents spanning all 11 GICS sectors (2018--2024) under a nine-window walk-forward protocol with 1,093 out-of-sample days, DyFO achieves strong predictive accuracy ($R^2 = 0.893$, Spearman $\rho = 0.958$, MAE $= 0.035$, classification F1 $= 0.793$), outperforming a recurrent Temporal Graph Network, a snapshot-based temporal baseline, and a static graph attention network across all windows. Diebold--Mariano tests with Newey--West HAC correction indicate statistically significant prediction-level gains, with all comparisons surviving Holm--Bonferroni correction. As a secondary diagnostic analysis, we also evaluate whether improved correlation forecasts translate into Global Minimum Variance portfolio performance. Paired block-bootstrap tests show that Sharpe-ratio differences are not statistically distinguishable, suggesting that classical mean--variance allocation may not directly exploit improvements in covariance forecast quality. Overall, DyFO contributes a relation-aware, bounded-history temporal graph architecture for financial co-movement forecasting, while highlighting the need for decision-aware allocation mechanisms to convert forecasting gains into portfolio utility.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}


Forecasting financial co-movements is a central problem in empirical finance, risk modelling, and covariance estimation. Accurate estimates of future
asset-pair correlations are required for understanding market structure, monitoring systemic risk, stress testing portfolios, and constructing
downstream allocation or hedging strategies. Traditional approaches rely on rolling-window Pearson correlations or parametric models such as the Dynamic Conditional Correlation GARCH (DCC-GARCH) of~\cite{engle2002dynamic}. Although effective at capturing volatility clustering, these methods treat each pair of assets largely in isolation and therefore cannot exploit the rich relational structure that exists among companies, such as sector membership, shared factor exposures, or common responses to market events.

Recent years have witnessed growing interest in graph-based models for financial markets. Several works have represented companies as nodes and
inter-company relations as edges, using graph neural networks to improve asset representations for prediction or portfolio construction. Notable
examples include relational graph convolutional architectures for portfolio management and cross-asset attention mechanisms for stock selection. While these approaches demonstrate the value of relational information, many of them operate on static graphs or optimise downstream trading decisions directly. As a result, they do not explicitly target the causally valid forecasting of the full next-day correlation matrix, nor do they isolate whether graph-based representations improve covariance prediction itself.

In this paper we introduce DyFO (Dynamic Financial Observer), a relation-aware temporal graph architecture for next-day pairwise correlation
forecasting. DyFO operates on a dynamic heterogeneous financial graph whose edges encode three complementary sources of co-movement information:
DCC-GARCH correlations (CORR), GICS sector links (SECT), and Fama--French five-factor co-loadings (FACT). Market activity is modelled as a continuous
stream of typed events, including price updates, earnings releases, macro surprises, and Federal Reserve decisions. A non-recurrent Temporal Graph
Attention Network (TGAT)~\cite{xu2020inductive} encodes recent events through bounded-history attention over per-node event buffers, while a relation-aware graph attention layer aggregates information over typed structural neighbourhoods. The resulting node embeddings are decoded into next-day pairwise correlation forecasts.

Our empirical results show that DyFO improves prediction-level accuracy over recurrent, snapshot-based, and static graph baselines across all nine
walk-forward windows. The improvement is confirmed by statistical tests on out-of-sample correlation forecasting errors. We additionally conduct a
secondary downstream evaluation using Global Minimum Variance portfolios. This analysis shows that Sharpe-ratio differences are not statistically
significant under paired block bootstrap, indicating that improved covariance forecasts do not automatically translate into portfolio gains when coupled with a classical mean--variance optimiser. We therefore frame DyFO primarily as a correlation forecasting architecture, with portfolio allocation left as a decision-aware extension for future work.

The main contributions of this work are threefold:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose a domain-adapted Temporal Graph Attention Network (TGAT)~\cite{xu2020inductive} for continuous-time financial event
    streams, extending the original homogeneous formulation with three key innovations: (i) typed edge embeddings that distinguish correlation,
    sector, and factor relations; (ii) a heterogeneous event vocabulary with domain-specific feature vectors; and (iii) a continuous regression decoder for next-day pairwise correlation forecasting. The resulting non-recurrent architecture avoids recurrent memory updates and backpropagation through time, while enabling accurate predictions on a dynamic heterogeneous graph.

    \item We integrate three complementary edge types---dynamic correlations, sector membership, and factor co-loadings---and demonstrate through ablation that each contributes measurably to predictive accuracy.

    \item We provide a statistically grounded evaluation of graph-based correlation forecasting models via Diebold--Mariano tests, Wilcoxon
    signed-rank tests, Newey--West HAC standard errors, and a secondary block-bootstrap portfolio diagnostic, showing that prediction-level gains
    are robust even when downstream GMV Sharpe differences are not statistically distinguishable.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work on graph-based and temporal models for portfolio management. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} formalises the problem and details the DyFO architecture. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} presents the experimental setup and main results. Finally, Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes and outlines future research directions.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

Portfolio management has traditionally been addressed through two complementary lines of research: classical optimisation techniques and, more recently, data-driven learning methods.

\paragraph{Classical approaches.}
Markowitz's mean-variance framework \cite{markowitz1952} remains the foundation of modern portfolio theory, selecting assets that minimise variance for a target return. Subsequent refinements introduced shrinkage estimators, robust optimisation, and factor models. The Fama--French five-factor model \cite{fama2015fivefactor} is now widely used to capture systematic risk exposures. For dynamic settings, Engle's DCC-GARCH model \cite{engle2002dynamic} provides time-varying conditional correlations that are still considered a strong baseline in the industry. These methods, however, treat pairwise relationships in isolation and cannot exploit higher-order relational structures among companies.

%\paragraph{Deep reinforcement learning for portfolio management.}

Subsequent improvements include the Portfolio Policy Network (PPN) \cite{zhang2020cost}, which adds a temporal correlational convolution block, and State-Augmented RL (SARL) \cite{ye2020reinforcement}, which augments states with external signals such as financial news. While effective, these approaches rely solely on price histories and ignore explicit inter-company relationships.

%\paragraph{Graph-based methods in finance.}

The Relational Stock Ranking (RSR) model \cite{feng2019temporal} proposed a temporal graph convolution that jointly models price evolution and static relations. Hierarchical Attention Networks for Stock prediction (HATS) \cite{kim2019hatshierarchicalgraphattention} and Multi-GCGRU \cite{ye2020multi} further enriched node representations with industry, shareholding, and topicality edges. More recently, DeepPocket \cite{soleymani2021deeppocket} applied a standard GCN over financial instruments, while the GPM framework of \cite{shi2022gpm} combined multi-scale temporal convolutions with an R-GCN over heterogeneous company relations (sector-industry and Wikidata links) and introduced temperature-scaled softmax for portfolio diversity.

\section{Methodology}\label{sec:methodology}

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:dyfo_overview}, DyFO combines event-driven temporal updates with heterogeneous graph structure to predict next-day pairwise correlations. The model constructs typed event messages, summarises each node's recent event history via temporal attention over per-node ring buffers, computes node embeddings through a \emph{relation-aware} heterogeneous graph attention layer, and finally decodes pairwise correlations for downstream portfolio evaluation.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{figures/dyfo_framework.png}
\caption{Overview of the DyFO architecture. Heterogeneous financial events are encoded into typed messages and stored in per-node fixed-capacity ring buffers. A multi-head temporal attention mechanism~\cite{xu2020inductive} summarises each node’s recent event history from a fixed-capacity ring buffer, producing a feedforward temporal representation without recurrent memory updates. These temporal summaries, together with time-varying node features and the heterogeneous graph structure (CORR, SECT, FACT edges), are processed by a relation-aware graph attention layer that conditions attention weights on typed edge embeddings, aggregating structural neighbourhood information. The resulting node embeddings are decoded into next-day pairwise correlation forecasts via a 3-layer MLP with $\tanh$ activation.}
\label{fig:dyfo_overview}
\end{figure}


\subsection{Problem Formulation}\label{problem-formulation-1}

Let $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$ be a heterogeneous financial graph where each node $v_i \in \mathcal{V}$ represents a publicly traded asset. We model market activity as a continuous stream of typed events $\{(s, r, t, \mathbf{f}_e)\}$, where $s, r \in \mathcal{V}$ are the source and (optional) target nodes, $t \in \mathbb{R}^+$ is a continuous timestamp, and $\mathbf{f}_e \in \mathbb{R}^{d_e}$ is an event feature vector.

The learning objective is next-day correlation prediction: given all events up to and including day $t$, predict the pairwise Pearson correlation $\rho_{ij}^{t+1}$ for all asset pairs $(i, j)$ on day $t+1$. This formulation is causally valid by construction --- no label information from day $t+1$ is visible at inference time.

\subsection{Correlation Target (DCC-GARCH)}\label{correlation-target-dcc-garch-1}

Correlation labels are estimated via the Dynamic Conditional Correlation (DCC) - Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity GARCH(1,1) model of Engle (2002), fitted over a rolling 252-day window. Let $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t$ be the vector of standardised GARCH(1,1) residuals. The conditional correlation matrix follows:

\begin{subequations}
\begin{equation}
Q_t = (1 - a - b)\,\bar{Q} + a\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{t-1}\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{t-1}^\top + b\,Q_{t-1}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
R_t = \mathrm{diag}(Q_t)^{-1/2}\,Q_t\,\mathrm{diag}(Q_t)^{-1/2}
\end{equation}
\end{subequations}
where $\bar{Q}$ is the unconditional covariance matrix estimated over the training window. DCC-GARCH is preferred over rolling Pearson because it captures volatility clustering and provides time-varying second-moment estimates robust to heteroskedastic equity returns.

For the event stream, \texttt{CORRELATION\_UPDATE} events are emitted whenever $|\rho_{ij}^t| \geq 0.3$ (sparsification threshold). Regression labels use the unsparsified DCC series to preserve continuous
signal for all pairs.

The central node \(v_i\) receives two classes of information:  \emph{(i)} event-driven messages (solid arrows) from neighbouring nodes, each encoding node states, a Time2Vec time gap \(\phi(\Delta t)\), event features \(\mathbf{f}_e\), and typed edge/event embeddings; these messages populate the per-node ring buffer and are summarised into \(\mathbf{s}_i(t)\) via temporal multi-head attention. \emph{(ii)} Structural context (dashed arrows) from typed graph edges ({\scshape corr}, {\scshape sect}, {\scshape fact}), whose 16-dimensional embeddings \(\mathbf{e}_{ij}\) are concatenated into the attention logit of the relation-aware GATConv layer, allowing the model to suppress attention towards redundant neighbours (e.g.\ dense {\scshape sect} edges) and amplify it towards informative ones (e.g.\ {\scshape corr}, {\scshape fact}).%
}
\label{fig:dyfo_message_passing}
\end{figure}


DyFO builds on the non-recurrent Temporal Graph Attention Network (TGAT) of~\cite{xu2020inductive}, which replaces recurrent node-memory updates with direct temporal attention over recent interactions stored in bounded per-node event buffers. DyFO extends the original TGAT---designed for homogeneous graphs with a single edge type and binary link prediction---in four key respects: (i)~the temporal attention mechanism receives typed edge embeddings ($\mathbf{e}_{\text{edge}} \in \mathbb{R}^{16}$) and event-type embeddings ($\mathbf{e}_{\text{event}} \in \mathbb{R}^{16}$) in the message function, enabling the model to learn type-specific temporal patterns ---for instance, distinguishing the informational impact of an earnings report from that of a macro surprise; (ii)~node features are 20-dimensional time-varying vectors (Sect.~\ref{node-features-1}) rather than static identifiers; (iii)~the structural graph attention layer is \emph{relation-aware}: attention weights are conditioned on typed edge embeddings via a dedicated projection matrix $\mathbf{W}_e$, preventing homogeneous treatment of structurally heterogeneous neighbour types (Sect.~\ref{sec:ablation} quantifies the effect of this conditioning); and (iv)~the decoder targets continuous pairwise correlations in $[-1,1]$ via $\tanh$ activation rather than binary link prediction. The complete pipeline per trading day $t$ is described below.

\textbf{Event Ring Buffer.} Each node maintains a fixed-capacity ring buffer of its most recent events. Rather than propagating a recurrent memory vector, recent historical events are attended to directly at inference time.

\textbf{Message Function.} For each event $(i, j, t, \mathbf{f}_e)$, a raw message for the source node is:

\begin{equation}\mathbf{m}_i(t) = \left[\mathbf{v}_i(t^-) \;\|\; \mathbf{v}_j(t^-) \;\|\;
\phi(\Delta t_i) \;\|\; \mathbf{f}_e \;\|\; \mathbf{e}_{\text{edge}} \;\|\;
\mathbf{e}_{\text{event}}\right] \in \mathbb{R}^{175}
\end{equation}

where $\phi(\Delta t) \in \mathbb{R}^{100}$ is the Time2Vec encoding~\cite{kazemi2019time2vec} of the elapsed time since node $i$'s last event, and $\mathbf{v}_j(t^-)$ is replaced with $\mathbf{0}$ for node-only events (target $= -1$). A symmetric message is computed for the target node $j$.

\textbf{Temporal Attention over Buffer.} The network applies a multi-head temporal attention mechanism over the messages stored in the event ring buffer. This produces a feedforward summary representation of the node’s recent temporal history. Thus, DyFO is not stateless in the strict sense, since it retains a bounded event buffer, but it is free from recurrent hidden-state updates and backpropagation through time.

\textbf{Unified Aggregation.} Source and target summary vectors are pooled and aggregated via mean, ensuring that nodes appearing as both source and target within the same daily batch receive a single coherent context representation. The mean aggregator is mandatory for broadcast events such as
\texttt{FED\_DECISION}, where all $N$ nodes receive an event at the identical timestamp; using a \texttt{last}-based aggregator in this setting would introduce order-dependent, non-deterministic updates.

\textbf{Relation-Aware Graph Attention Embedding.}
Node embeddings are computed by a single-layer multi-head graph attention network (GATConv 2 heads) over the static heterogeneous neighbourhood. Critically, the attention weight between node $i$ and neighbour $j$ is
conditioned on the typed edge embedding $\mathbf{e}_{ij} \in \mathbb{R}^{16}$:

\begin{equation}
\alpha_{ij} = \mathrm{softmax}_j \!\left(
    \mathrm{LeakyReLU}\!\left(
        \mathbf{a}^{\top}
        \left[
            \mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_i \;\|\;
            \mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j \;\|\;
            \mathbf{W}_e \mathbf{e}_{ij}
        \right]
    \right)
\right)
\label{eq:relation_aware_attn}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{W}$, $\mathbf{W}_e$, and $\mathbf{a}$ are learned parameters,
and $\mathbf{h}_i = [\mathbf{s}_i(t) \,\|\, \mathbf{v}_i(t)] \in \mathbb{R}^{192}$
is the concatenation of the temporal summary and the time-varying node feature
vector. The final node embedding is:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_i(t) = \mathrm{MLP}\!\left(\mathbf{h}_i \,\|\,
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} \alpha_{ij}\,\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_j
\right) \in \mathbb{R}^{100}
\end{equation}
This relation-aware formulation resolves an attention-dilution failure mode that arises under standard homogeneous graph attention. Without edge conditioning, the GATConv layer treats edges of type \texttt{CORR}, \texttt{SECT}, and \texttt{FACT} as structurally identical neighbours. When the static \texttt{SECT} edges (dense, binary) are added to the graph alongside the more informative \texttt{CORR} and \texttt{FACT} edges, the increased node degree dilutes the attention mass directed towards discriminative neighbours, degrading predictive performance ($\Delta R^2 = -0.0042$ for \textsc{all\_edges} vs.\\textsc{CORR+FACT} under the homogeneous formulation; see Sect.~\ref{sec:ablation}). Equation~\eqref{eq:relation_aware_attn} corrects this by allowing the model to learn relation-specific attention biases, so that redundant static neighbours receive low weight automatically.

\textbf{Decoder.} A 3-layer MLP with architecture $[200 \to 64 \to 32 \to 1]$ takes the concatenated pair embedding $[\mathbf{z}_i \,\|\, \mathbf{z}_j] \in \mathbb{R}^{200}$ and outputs $\hat{\rho}_{ij}^{t+1} \in [-1, 1]$ via a $\tanh$ final activation.

\textbf{Weight Initialisation.} Linear layers use Xavier uniform initialisation, standardising gradient dynamics early in training for the attention components.

The total parameter count is \textbf{85{,}480}.\footnote{Computed via \texttt{sum(p.numel() for p in model.parameters())}. The substantial reduction relative to the recurrent TGN baseline (556{,}909 parameters) reflects the removal of the GRU memory updater and the replacement of memory-dimensional message inputs ($d_m = 172$) with node-feature inputs ($d_v = 20$).}

\subsection{Training Protocol}\label{training-protocol-1}

\textbf{Pre-training objective.} The model is trained to minimise the Huber loss (SmoothL1, $\delta = 1$) between predicted and DCC-GARCH correlations over all known pairs on day $t+1$:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}_t|} \sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{P}_t}
\ell_\delta\!\left(\hat{\rho}_{ij}^{t+1},\, \rho_{ij}^{t+1}\right)
\end{equation}

Huber loss is chosen for robustness to outlier correlations near $\pm 1$ that arise during market stress periods.

\textbf{Walk-forward split \& Evaluation.} The full period is partitioned into nine non-overlapping walk-forward windows (500 train / 125 validation / 125 test days, step 125 days), yielding 1{,}093 total out-of-sample trading days. The event ring buffer state is \textbf{not} reset between splits within a window; each window is initialised from scratch to ensure strict causal validity. To establish statistical rigour for portfolio metrics, we apply a \textbf{Block Bootstrap} methodology ($B = 2{,}000$ re-samples, block size 5 days) to the out-of-sample returns, providing 95\% confidence intervals and $p$-values for pairwise comparisons.

\textbf{Optimisation.} Adam optimiser with learning rate $\eta = 10^{-3}$ and early stopping (patience 10 epochs for DyFO and TGN, 5 for baselines) monitoring validation $R^2$; the best checkpoint is restored for test evaluation. Gradients are clipped to $\ell_2$-norm $\leq 0.5$. Maximum 50 training epochs per window.

\textbf{Reproducibility.} All experiments are run with a fixed random seed per window to ensure deterministic data splits. Data preparation (downloads, DCC-GARCH estimation) is performed once and shared across model variants to isolate stochasticity to model initialisation.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}\label{evaluation-metrics}

We report the following metrics computed on the held-out test portions of the nine non-overlapping walk-forward windows (125 test days per window, 1{,}093 out-of-sample trading days in total).


\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{R²} (coefficient of determination): fraction of variance in   \(\rho_{ij}^{t+1}\) explained by \(\hat{\rho}_{ij}^{t+1}\).
\item
  \textbf{Spearman \(\rho\)}: rank correlation between predicted and actual correlations, measuring ordinal accuracy of the predicted correlation structure.
\item
  \textbf{MAE}: mean absolute error in correlation units.
\end{itemize}

Additionally, we report a derived classification metric, F1 score at $|\hat{\rho}| \geq 0.5$, denoted as cls-F1, which reflects the practical accuracy of identifying high-correlation asset pairs.

\subsection{Recurrent Memory Instability vs.\ Attention}\label{training-stability}

The recurrent TGN baseline exhibited systematic instability across the nine non-overlapping walk-forward windows. Specifically, the GRU memory mechanism caused $R^2$ to collapse to $0.623$ in Window 7 (Spearman $\rho = 0.865$), a degradation of $\Delta R^2 = -0.23$ relative to its adjacent windows, coinciding with the abrupt 2022--2023 market regime transition. This collapse is attributable to memory ``cold starts'' at window boundaries---the mismatch between the state accumulated during training and the drastically different dynamics encountered at the onset of a new regime. The instability persisted despite gradient clipping ($\ell_2 \leq 0.5$), linear warmup, and orthogonal GRU initialisation, indicating that the failure mode is architectural rather than optimisation-related. The result is a standard deviation of $R^2 = 0.077$ across windows for TGN, more than twice the TGAT's $\sigma_{R^2} = 0.034$.

The TGAT architecture avoids this failure mode entirely by replacing the unbounded recurrent memory with strict multi-head attention over a finite event ring buffer. Because the buffer contents are always grounded in recent observed events, the representation cannot drift into pathological states during regime transitions or at window boundaries, establishing robust structural consistency across all nine evaluation windows. Furthermore, the ROLAND baseline exhibited near-complete classification failure (F1 $= 0.00$) in seven of nine windows, predicting only the majority class---a consequence of its monthly snapshot granularity being insufficient to track the day-to-day correlation dynamics required for the task.

\paragraph{Statistical significance — Wilcoxon signed-rank test.}
To assess whether differences in predictive accuracy across configurations are statistically reliable, we applied the Wilcoxon signed-rank test to the nine-window MSE vectors (Fig~\ref{fig:wilcoxon_dotplot}). This non-parametric surrogate is appropriate given that the walk-forward evaluation yields monthly synthetic reports (windowed MSE means) rather than the high-frequency residual sequences required by the Diebold-Mariano test.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/wilcoxon_pvalues_dotplot.png}
    \caption{Dot plot of Wilcoxon signed-rank test $p$-values across model
    comparisons ($n=9$). The vertical dashed line denotes the 5\% statistical
    significance threshold.}
    \label{fig:wilcoxon_dotplot}
\end{figure}

The key findings are as follows. First, \textsc{all\_edges} significantly outperforms \textsc{CORR-only} ($p = 0.0039$), confirming that price co-movement alone is insufficient for optimal portfolio attention allocation. Second, the addition of factor loadings (\textsc{CORR+FACT}) confers a substantial and statistically significant improvement over correlation-only edges ($p = 0.0078$), whereas sector membership (\textsc{CORR+SECT}) yields only a marginal, non-significant gain ($p = 0.0742$). Third, the full graph (\textsc{all\_edges}) and \textsc{CORR+FACT} are statistically indistinguishable at the aggregate level ($p = 0.25$), with \textsc{all\_edges} achieving the highest mean R\textsuperscript{2} (0.8926 vs.\ 0.8904) and the lowest MAE (0.0351 vs.\ 0.0356), while \textsc{CORR+FACT} attains a marginally higher Sharpe proxy (1.610 vs.\ 1.601).
These results validate the full heterogeneous graph as the theoretically grounded and empirically competitive choice for DyFO's primary configuration.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}\label{sec:conclusion}

%In this paper, we introduced DyFO (Dynamic Financial Observer), a fully stateless Temporal Graph Attention Network (TGAT) designed for next-day pairwise correlation forecasting in financial markets. The proposed framework integrates three complementary relational sources---dynamic correlations (CORR), sector links (SECT), and factor co-loadings (FACT)---within a heterogeneous graph, while incorporating asynchronous market events through a continuous-time message-passing mechanism. By combining temporal attention over event histories, typed structural relations, and heterogeneous graph attention, DyFO provides a fully stateless and causally valid architecture for modelling evolving co-movement structure across assets.

%Empirically, DyFO achieves strong and consistent predictive performance under a rigorous nine-window walk-forward protocol spanning 1{,}093 out-of-sample days across heterogeneous market regimes (2018--2024, $N=50$ assets). The model consistently outperformed all baselines across prediction-level metrics ($R^2 = 0.893$, Spearman $\rho = 0.958$, MAE $= 0.035$, classification F1 $= 0.793$), maintaining $R^2 > 0.83$ in every window while the recurrent TGN collapsed to $R^2 = 0.623$ during a regime transition. Pooled Diebold--Mariano tests with Newey--West HAC correction yield effect sizes of $|d| > 2.2$ against all baselines, with all results surviving Holm--Bonferroni adjustment. Notably, ROLAND exhibited near-complete classification failure (F1 $\approx 0$) in seven of nine windows, underscoring that snapshot-based granularity is insufficient for daily correlation dynamics.

%At the same time, the portfolio-level evaluation revealed a more nuanced picture. Although DyFO produced substantially more accurate correlation forecasts, these gains did not translate into statistically significant improvements in downstream GMV portfolio Sharpe ratios under paired block bootstrap evaluation. The coefficient of variation of the Sharpe Proxy exceeded 90\% for every model---confirming that market regime, not forecast quality, dominates portfolio outcomes under mean-variance allocation. This result is consistent with the well-documented error-amplification pathology of Markowitz optimisation~\cite{michaud1989markowitz,DeMiguel2009Optimal}, which identifies the optimizer, not the encoder, as the bottleneck.

We introduced DyFO, a non-recurrent relation-aware temporal graph attention architecture for next-day pairwise correlation forecasting over a heterogeneous financial graph. Under a nine-window walk-forward protocol covering 1,093 out-of-sample days and 50 S\&P 500 constituents, DyFO achieved strong and stable predictive performance, outperforming recurrent, snapshot-based, and static graph baselines across correlation-level metrics. The results support the central claim of this work: typed-edge conditioning and bounded-history temporal attention provide an effective architecture for
financial co-movement forecasting.

A secondary Global Minimum Variance portfolio diagnostic showed that prediction-level improvements did not yield statistically significant Sharpe-ratio gains under paired block bootstrap. Rather than undermining the forecasting contribution, this result highlights a known gap between covariance prediction accuracy and realised portfolio utility under classical mean--variance optimisation. Future work should therefore investigate decision-aware allocation frameworks, differentiable optimisation layers, and reinforcement-learning allocators designed to exploit richer covariance
forecasts directly. Additional extensions include incorporating textual financial news, analyst revisions, and larger asset universes.

Overall, DyFO should be understood as a methodological contribution to graph-based financial correlation forecasting, not as a complete end-to-end portfolio allocation system.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} To support open science and facilitate the replication of our results, the complete source code, hyperparameter configurations, pre-processing scripts, and instructions for data acquisition have been made available in an anonymous GitHub repository at \url{https://github.com/anonymous-submission-dyfo/dyfo-bracis}.

%These findings lead to several directions for future research. First, the portfolio construction stage should be revisited through decision-aware allocation frameworks, such as differentiable optimization layers or reinforcement-learning-based allocators, which may be better suited to exploit richer covariance forecasts. Second, the heterogeneous graph can be extended with additional relation types, provided they remain economically interpretable and statistically justified. Third, the event stream may be enriched with alternative sources of exogenous information, such as textual financial news, macro narratives, or analyst revisions. Finally, future work should investigate larger asset universes, longer test horizons, and alternative portfolio objectives beyond GMV, including risk-aware and utility-based formulations, in order to better assess when superior correlation prediction translates into downstream financial value.

%Overall, this work shows that stateless temporal graph attention constitutes a rigorous and robust approach for financial co-movement forecasting. DyFO narrows the gap between dynamic relational learning and practical covariance modelling, while also highlighting an important lesson: improving predictive accuracy is necessary, but not sufficient, for improving portfolio decisions unless the downstream allocation mechanism is designed to make effective use of that predictive signal.
